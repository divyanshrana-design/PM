Pick a methodology.
Not a religion.

Agile purists and Waterfall diehards will tell you theirs is the one true way. For most Irish small businesses, that's nonsense. Pick the right tool for the job.

Deep dive

The five approaches, in plain English

Agile

FlexibleIterative

"We'll figure it out as we go, but properly."

Agile isn't really a methodology. It's a mindset built on four values: collaboration, working software (or deliverables) over documentation, customer partnership, and responding to change.

In practice, teams deliver value in small, fast increments (often called iterations or sprints), then review and adjust. Scrum and Kanban are the two most common ways to implement it.

Great for
  • Software and digital products
  • Marketing campaigns
  • Projects with changing customer needs
  • Creative work
Watch out
  • Needs engaged stakeholders. No "throw it over the wall"
  • Harder to predict total cost/timeline upfront
  • Teams can drift without clear goals

Waterfall

SequentialPredictable

"Finish one phase completely before starting the next."

The oldest approach, born in the 1970s. Requirements, design, build, test, launch, then maintain. Going back to a previous phase is expensive, which is both its strength (forces discipline) and its weakness (no flexibility).

Great for
  • Construction and physical builds
  • Highly regulated projects (health, legal)
  • Fixed price client work with clear scope
  • Teams that want absolute clarity
Watch out
  • Customer doesn't see anything until the end
  • Changes are costly
  • Risks pile up if testing is left to the final phase

Scrum

Agile frameworkSprints

"Small, repeating 2 week cycles, with defined roles and ceremonies."

The most popular way to "do Agile." Teams work in sprints of 1 to 4 weeks, with three defined roles (Scrum Master, Product Owner, Development Team) and four recurring meetings (sprint planning, daily standup, sprint review, retrospective).

Great for
  • Small software or product teams (5 to 9 people)
  • Teams that can dedicate time to ceremonies
  • Complex work with high uncertainty
Watch out
  • Overkill for a 2 to 3 person team
  • Ceremonies can become ritual without the why
  • Roles can feel heavy in a small business

Kanban

VisualContinuous flow

"Make work visible. Limit work in progress. Keep it flowing."

Originally from Toyota's factories. A Kanban board has columns, typically To Do, Doing, Done, and each task is a card that moves left to right. The magic is in WIP limits: you cap how many things can be "in progress" at once, which forces you to finish before starting.

The simplest, most SME friendly method. If you're starting from nothing, start here.

Great for
  • Continuous work (support, content, ops)
  • Small teams and solo operators
  • Remote/distributed teams
  • Charities juggling many small tasks
Watch out
  • Less useful for big, deadline driven projects
  • Without WIP limits, it's just a to do list

Hybrid Most popular

Pragmatic

"Take the structure of Waterfall, the flexibility of Agile, and ignore the purists."

The fastest growing approach, up 57.5% since 2020. Most real Irish SMEs end up here naturally. Typical hybrid: Waterfall style planning (fixed budget, fixed deadline), then Scrum or Kanban during execution so the team can adapt.

Scrumban is a popular hybrid. Scrum's sprint cycles combined with Kanban's visual workflow and WIP limits.

Great for
  • Client projects with fixed budgets
  • Grant funded charity programmes
  • Marketing + product launch combinations
  • Any team with mixed project types
Watch out
  • Needs a clear owner, or it can become "we do whatever we like"
  • Document your chosen approach so new staff don't drown
