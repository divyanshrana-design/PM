Chapter 1

The fundamentals, in plain English.

If you've ever organised a charity fun run, launched a café, or moved the office, you've done project management. You just didn't call it that. Here's the proper toolkit.

What is project management?

Project management is simply using your knowledge, skills, tools and techniques to finish a specific piece of work within agreed time, money and quality limits. That's it.

A project is a piece of work with a clear start and end, unlike your day to day operations, which just keep going. Launching a new product is a project. Serving customers every day is an operation. Opening a second charity shop is a project. Running the existing shop is an operation.

Every project answers three questions:

  1. What needs to be done?
  2. How are we going to do it?
  3. When must it be finished?

The small business translation

You don't need 200 pages of methodology. You need clear outcomes, honest deadlines, and the shortest path between them. That's the whole game.

Project management vs. "just running the business"

New business owners often ask: isn't this just management? Close, but different.

  Project management General management
FocusA specific piece of work with a deliverableOngoing operations, whatever today brings
DurationHas a start and an end dateContinues indefinitely
AuthorityLimited to the project team, often across departmentsBroader, managing people and P&L
GoalDeliver on time, on budget, to qualitySustained growth and performance

Why it matters more in 2026

Three things have changed:

  • The global project management software market will pass $15bn in 2025, most of that spend is from small and mid sized teams, not enterprises.
  • The hybrid way of working has grown 57.5% since 2020. You're coordinating with people in different rooms, houses and sometimes countries. Tribal knowledge doesn't cut it anymore.
  • AI has collapsed the cost of doing project admin. A team of five can now run projects like a team of fifty could five years ago, if they use it.

For Irish SMEs and charities in particular, structured project management is no longer a "nice to have." It's how you win grants, bid for contracts, report to funders, and compete with better resourced organisations.

The 5 phases of any project

Every project, from your daughter's wedding to building the Aviva Stadium, moves through the same five phases. The depth you go into each changes. The sequence doesn't.

1 Initiation

Is this project worth doing at all?

The cheapest projects are the ones you kill in this phase. Before you spend a euro, you decide whether the idea matches your goals, whether you can actually do it, and who needs to be involved.

You leave this phase with a one page project charter: the problem, the proposed solution, rough scope, rough budget, and who's in charge.

Irish SME example

A Cork café owner wants to add a weekend brunch menu. Initiation = costing out ingredients, checking staffing, confirming the POS system can handle it, and writing a 5 line "why we're doing this" note. 2 hours of work. Saves a month of chaos.

2 Planning

How exactly are we going to do this?

Traditional projects spend nearly half of their total time in planning. For a small SME project you won't, but don't skimp entirely. Planning is where you find problems while they're still cheap.

A usable plan has:

  • A work breakdown structure, the project chopped into tasks and subtasks
  • A schedule with milestones and deadlines
  • A resource list of people, money and tools
  • A communication plan that says who hears what, and when
  • A risk register that lists what could go wrong and what you'll do about it
Planning isn't paperwork. It's solving problems before they show up.

3 Execution

Doing the actual work.

This is the longest phase and where most small teams spend 80% of their time. Your job as the project lead shifts from planning to unblocking:

  • Coordinating people and resources
  • Running the processes you planned
  • Producing the actual deliverables
  • Handling change requests as they arrive
  • Keeping the team motivated. Celebrating small wins matters more than you think

4 Monitoring & controlling

Are we still on track? If not, what do we change?

This phase runs in parallel with execution. Think of it as the project's radar (monitoring) and steering wheel (controlling).

  • Comparing actual progress to the plan
  • Watching the budget and resource use
  • Running quality checks
  • Spotting emerging risks early
  • Making course corrections before small issues become big ones

Where AI earns its keep

Most monitoring work is dull: reading updates, spotting trends, summarising status. All of it is handed off to AI beautifully, which is why the AI playbook is the next thing to read.

5 Closing

We're done. But are we really done?

Most teams skip closing. It costs them every time. A proper close:

  • Gets formal sign off from stakeholders and clients
  • Finishes admin: contracts closed, invoices paid, tools cancelled
  • Hands the outputs over to whoever runs them day to day
  • Captures a short lessons learned note, the single highest ROI 30 minutes in a project
  • Releases the team with a thank you

Without closing, you get scope creep that never ends, invoices that don't get paid, and the same mistakes repeated on the next project.

What a project manager actually does

In a small team, there often isn't a dedicated PM. The role is worn by the owner, a senior staffer, or whoever's best organised. Either way, the work is the same.

Core responsibilities

  • Define what "done" looks like
  • Plan and document the tasks
  • Manage the budget and resources
  • Lead and motivate the team
  • Monitor progress and handle risks
  • Ensure quality on the deliverables
  • Keep stakeholders happy and informed

The skills that matter most

Project managers spend around 90% of their time communicating. Communication is the skill. Everything else supports it.

Beyond that, the high value skills are:

  • Leadership. Inspiring a team that doesn't report to you
  • Time management. Yours and everyone else's
  • Critical thinking. The ability to step back and ask "is this still the right plan?"
  • Adaptability. No plan survives first contact with reality
  • Risk management. Seeing the problem before it arrives
  • Negotiation. With suppliers, clients, and your own team

Common mistakes to avoid

1. Skipping planning

Under pressure to "just start," teams jump straight to execution. Every time, they end up with missed deadlines, overspent budgets, and disappointed stakeholders. Planning isn't a one time thing, keep revisiting it.

2. Assuming people understand

Poor communication is the #1 reason projects fail. Set comms expectations early. How often, what channel, what format. Don't assume that silence means agreement.

3. Treating risk as somebody else's problem

Don't wait for problems to happen. Spend 30 minutes at the start listing what could go wrong, how likely it is, and what you'd do. Revisit weekly.

4. Relying on email, WhatsApp and memory

Knowledge workers lose 13.7 hours a week to scattered tools. That's 89 working days a year. Even a free tool like Trello or Notion beats running everything from your inbox.
